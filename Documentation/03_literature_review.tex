\chapter{Literature Review}

\section{Introduction}

This literature review aims to compile prior research on \acp{LLM}, inference deployment, \ac{RAG}-based architectures and evaluation methods related to this thesis. The increasing scale and complexity of \acp{LLM} are requiring ever greater amounts of memory, computation, latency, and operational costs when they are deployed. In contrast with these challenges, prior research on \ac{RAG} has demonstrated how external retrieval may enhance the ground-ability of the responses generated by models through linking the output of the model to a document repository.

As such, this chapter synthesizes prior research into three main categories: (i) approaches for deploying \acp{LLM} locally, cloud-based or using \acf{HPC} infrastructures; (ii) optimization strategies for inference, which impact the scalability and memory utilization of models; (iii) \ac{RAG} architectures that include document indexing, retrieval and response generation. Additionally, it reviews benchmarks and evaluation methodologies commonly employed to evaluate both the quality of models and the quality of retrievals as well as the overall performance of systems.

It is not in the intention of this review to discuss each optimization methodology at length, but rather to provide context for this thesis. Specifically, this thesis will compare six different \ac{RAG} pipeline configurations at the level of retrieval with respect to timing behaviour over both local and \ac{HPC} execution.

\section{LLM Deployment Approaches}

\subsection{Local and Cloud-Based Deployments}

Research into the deployment of \acp{LLM} often compares cloud-based infrastructure, local systems, and managed compute environments. The main differences between these types of deployments are related to their available resources (such as CPU/GPU), how scalable they can be, the total cost of ownership, and the performance characteristics of each environment.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Deployments using cloud-based infrastructure} utilize the elastic computing resources provided by major cloud vendors such as \acf{AWS}, Microsoft Azure, and Google Cloud. These services support the use of distributed inference engines, highly scalable \acp{GPU}, and also support the ability to manage model serving applications at scale, thus allowing for reliable execution of very large \ac{LLM} workloads. Additionally studies have shown that through automated scaling, and distributed storage a high degree of efficiency is possible when executing multi-billion parameter models \cite{narayanan2021cloudscale}. Some negative aspects associated with deploying models in the cloud include: the high operating expense (e.g. hourly charges for running the service), data-transfer fees, vendor lock-in, and network latency (the time it takes for data to travel over the network).
    \item \textbf{Deployments using local infrastructure} require a user to deploy their models to a workstation-class \ac{GPU} or an on-premises server. Local deployments offer several advantages including: predictable performance, low latency per user, and strong data governance. A significant disadvantage of local deployments is the inability to scale up and down to accommodate multiple users or meet the growing demand for increasingly larger \ac{LLM} models.
\end{itemize}

Given the current trade-offs between control (local deployments) and scalability (cloud deployments), \ac{HPC}-based deployments are relevant because they provide managed compute resources, scheduling, shared storage, and reproducible execution workflows that differ from both personal workstations and persistent cloud services.

\subsection{HPC-Based Deployments}

\acf{HPC} systems offer a potential solution for scaling \ac{LLM} workloads because they provide clustered compute resources, high-bandwidth connectivity, and large overall memory capacity. In contrast to cloud-based environments optimized for loosely connected service-based applications, \ac{HPC} clusters allow for rapid communication between each node, enabling both efficient distributed training and inference \cite{dongarra2020hpcsurvey}.

A great deal of prior work focuses on distributed training frameworks (Megatron-LM \cite{shoeybi2019megatron}, DeepSpeed's ZeRO \cite{rajbhandari2020deepspeed}) that utilize tensor, pipeline, and data parallelism to enable the training of extremely large models. These approaches also support distributed inference for models whose memory requirements exceed the capacity of a single device.

In addition to the above references, other researchers have examined aspects of deep learning in \ac{HPC} environments including high-speed interconnects such as InfiniBand and NVLink, the effects of parallel file system \ac{I/O} performance on \ac{LLM}, and the impact of scheduler overhead on performance. These studies show that hardware, storage, and scheduling decisions can affect machine-learning workloads. For this thesis, those same categories are used to interpret retrieval-based workloads, where retrieval latency and storage throughput affect overall \ac{RAG} performance.

Prior research has shown that system-level decisions, including containerization with Apptainer and workload scheduling with Slurm, can improve the efficiency and scalability of different Machine Learning workloads. This thesis applies the same type of systems-aware reasoning to the implemented \ac{LLM} inference and \ac{RAG} deployment workflow.

Although \ac{HPC} platforms can support larger and more controlled workloads than a laptop, the relevant point for this thesis is the practical comparison between local execution and managed \ac{HPC} execution of the same \ac{RAG} workflow.

\subsection{Model Serving Frameworks}

A number of high-throughput model-serving frameworks have been developed to scale \acp{LLM}:

The \textbf{\acf{vLLM}} framework introduced the PagedAttention mechanism to improve \ac{KV}-cache management and increase throughput under concurrent request workloads \cite{kwon2023vllm}. In this thesis, \ac{vLLM} is relevant because it is used to serve the generation, embedding, and reranking components in the \ac{HPC} workflow.

The \textbf{NVIDIA Triton Inference Server} is an inference server framework capable of running multiple frameworks concurrently using dynamic batch sizing and GPU-aware task scheduling. However, it was not initially designed with \acp{LLM} in mind; therefore, it has limited ability to optimize \ac{LLM}-specific parameters.

The \textbf{\acf{TGI}} framework was created for the purpose of optimizing performance when deploying transformer-based text generation models. \ac{TGI} supports tensor-parallelized inference, quantization, and continuous batch processing.

\textbf{RayServe} is a distributed orchestration tool for scaling applications across cluster resources. While RayServe does provide a flexible way to deploy applications across multiple machines, its primary function is focused on the distribution of tasks across nodes rather than providing optimizations for inference speed. 

Therefore, while several serving frameworks can be used to distribute application logic, this thesis focuses on the practical role of \ac{vLLM}-based serving in the implemented \ac{HPC} workflow and on how deployment overheads are separated from retrieval and generation timing.

\section{Optimization Techniques for LLM Inference}

\subsection{Quantization and Pruning}

Quantization reduces memory usage and computational cost by converting model weights and activations from higher-precision formats such as \ac{FP32} or \ac{FP16} to lower-precision formats such as \ac{INT8} or \ac{INT4}. Methods including LLM.int8 \cite{dettmers2022int8}, \ac{GPTQ}, and \ac{AWQ} allow for significant reductions in resource utilization with minimal losses in accuracy. This can enable larger batch sizes and faster inference.

Pruning is a method to remove redundant weights from an existing model to reduce the size of the model and computational complexity. Although early research focused primarily on vision models, recent work has applied pruning to Transformer models, and has demonstrated that structured sparsity can be used to reduce compute requirements without large losses in accuracy \cite{frankle2019lottery}.

There are both advantages and disadvantages for using quantization and pruning in \ac{HPC} environments. These techniques can reduce memory use and support larger batch sizes, but they may also introduce accuracy or compatibility trade-offs. In this thesis, they are treated as background techniques rather than implemented experimental variables.

\subsection{Knowledge Distillation}

Distillation has been shown to allow a larger "teacher" \ac{LLM} to be reduced into a smaller "student". For example, models such as DistilBERT \cite{sanh2019distilbert} have demonstrated that many capabilities of an \ac{LLM} might be preserved in a model that is significantly cheaper than the original model. 

The primary advantage of using knowledge distillation to train a "student" neural network (i.e., one that learns from a larger pre-trained teacher) is that it results in a significant reduction in both latency and memory requirements. Distillation has no direct impact on knowledge grounding; therefore, improving retrieval accuracy, or reducing hallucinations are not addressed. As such, knowledge distillation was not considered an experimental variable for this study; rather, was viewed through the lens of the retrieval system's design and deployment.

\subsection{Parallelization and Sharding}

Strategies to enable Data Parallelism, Tensor Parallelism and Pipeline Parallelism, represent the most important ways to scale LLMs. Tensor-Parallelism and Pipeline-Parallelism as used in Megatron-LM \cite{shoeybi2019megatron} are key to enabling distributed execution. The use of parameter, gradient and optimizer state shards in DeepSpeed ZeRO \cite{rajbhandari2020deepspeed} and PyTorch \ac{FSDP} can be seen as an extension of the ideas behind those two forms of parallelization. 

Although, there is a substantial body of work analyzing the impact of these methods on the training process, this thesis treats parallelization and sharding mainly as deployment background. The implemented comparison focuses on the measurable behaviour of the \ac{RAG} workflow rather than on redesigning the language model execution engine.

\subsection{Caching and Memory Optimization}

FlashAttention is an attention optimization method that reduces memory movement and improves the efficiency of attention computation without changing the mathematical result of attention \cite{dao2022flashattention}.

The PagedAttention mechanism used by \ac{vLLM} splits the \ac{KV-cache} into paged blocks, which improves performance in systems that serve multiple requests at once \cite{kwon2023vllm}.

In addition to sparsification, eviction strategies and cache compressions, researchers are actively exploring other optimizations. However, most of them are still in an experimental phase.

Due to multi-user workloads and job schedulers, \ac{HPC} environments have varying amounts of available memory. For this thesis, caching and memory optimization are relevant because they influence model-serving behaviour and repeated execution costs, even though they are not the main experimental variables.

\section{RAG Systems and Optimization Strategies}

\subsection{Existing Frameworks}

The major frameworks for building RAG pipelines (LangChain \cite{langchain2023}, LlamaIndex \cite{llamaindex2023}, and Haystack) offer modules to build the pipeline. Their common role is to organize document loading, embedding, retrieval, prompt construction, and answer generation. This thesis uses this framework-oriented view to structure the implemented pipelines and keep retrieval choices comparable.

\subsection{Retrieval Efficiency and Hybrid Approaches}

The combination of sparse (like \ac{BM25}) and dense (like \ac{DPR}) retrieval techniques may be used in order to obtain a better recall rate than each single technique could offer on its own \cite{robertson2009bm25,karpukhin2020dpr}. Dense retrieval techniques have shown good results regarding query-document similarities, while sparse techniques show high quality for retrieving all the exact keywords mentioned in a query.

The evaluation of the hybrid retrieval strategy as a retrieval level variant has been carried out within the scope of this thesis. Its performance will be discussed together with timing behaviour since the use of both sparse and dense retrieval strategies is able to increase the number of retrieved relevant documents; however, they can also lead to an increased cost in terms of time needed by the system for finding them.

\subsection{RAG on HPC Environments}

RAG systems are often discussed in cloud computing or enterprise deployment settings, where services may exist persistently, dynamic auto-scaling may be available, and container orchestration may be part of the infrastructure.

In contrast, \ac{HPC} systems utilize batch scheduling (e.g., Slurm) and employ parallel storage systems (e.g. Lustre, \acs{GPFS}). The jobs executed on these systems do not remain active after their completion. These differences create additional complexities regarding how to serve, retrieve, and perform distributed inference \cite{slurm2003,kurtzer2017singularity,dongarra2020hpcsurvey,abdolrashidi2021large}.

\section{Benchmarking and Evaluation Studies}

Benchmarking methods such as \acs{MMLU}, \acs{HELM} \cite{liang2022helm} and the OpenLLM leaderboard evaluate \ac{LLM} performance. The MLPerf benchmark \cite{reddi2020mlperf}, however, is designed to measure inference performance. As such, these benchmarks represent a good reference point for evaluating \ac{LLM} performance; they do not, however, provide an adequate framework for the workflow level evaluations presented in this thesis.

As indicated above, evaluation of \ac{RAG} systems requires consideration of two key aspects of system performance. These include the overall quality of the retrieved answers (i.e., answer quality), and the quality of the actual retrieval process that generates those answers (i.e.,  retrieval quality). Specific benchmarks for \ac{RAG} systems, such as \acs{KILT}, evaluate the retrieval performance. In contrast, other evaluation frameworks focus on assessing the correctness, faithfulness, and relevance of the generated answers. The thesis will use a workflow-level evaluation method, which will compare the timing of the six implemented \ac{RAG} pipelines along with their respective answer quality and retrieval quality metrics, for the local implementation only.

Therefore, the evaluation provided in this thesis is different than general-purpose benchmark suites. Rather than ranking numerous models using many publicly available tasks, it evaluates a small controlled number of \ac{RAG} configuration options on the exact same corpus, under identical scenario-based conditions, and when compared locally versus \acf{HPC} based timing conditions.

\section{Positioning of This Thesis}

The reviewed literature motivates several design choices in this thesis:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \ac{LLM} deployment studies highlight the importance of hardware, serving frameworks, and resource management.
    \item \ac{RAG} studies highlight the importance of retrieval quality, grounding, and answer quality.
    \item \ac{HPC} deployment practices highlight the importance of scheduling, shared storage, containers, and reproducibility.
    \item This thesis combines these perspectives in a controlled comparison of six retrieval-level \ac{RAG} variants across a local and \ac{HPC} workflow.
\end{itemize}

The contribution is therefore practical and bounded: the thesis does not claim to replace broad benchmark suites or exhaust the literature, but it applies established concepts to a reproducible implementation and evaluates how retrieval choices affect timing, retrieval quality, and answer quality.

\section{Summary}

This chapter reviewed the literature on \ac{LLM} deployment strategies, inference optimization techniques, \ac{RAG} system design, and evaluation methods for \acp{LLM} and \ac{RAG}. The reviewed work shows that deployment environment, model-serving approach, retrieval design, and evaluation metrics all influence the behaviour of practical \ac{RAG} systems.

Therefore, chapter 4 uses these concepts to define the methodology for comparing the implemented \ac{RAG} pipelines, their deployment workflow, and the metrics used to evaluate timing, retrieval quality, and answer quality.
