\chapter{Results and Discussion}

\section{Introduction}

This chapter presents the outcomes from the two stages of execution (local and \ac{HPC}) of this work. The local stage is concerned with measuring the time for the three types of processing (retrieval, quality of answers and overall run-time). These are detailed within chapters 4 and 5. A comparative analysis will be undertaken to determine how well each of these stages has performed relative to one another in terms of their run-times.

At the local level it can be seen that the performance of the three elements of the pipeline (retrieval, quality of answers and run-time) do not necessarily progress alongside. At the \ac{HPC} level we will compare the run-time performances of all six variations of the pipeline in an alternative execution environment.

\subsection{Local Timing Comparison}

Figure~\ref{fig:timing-breakdown} illustrates the average execution time for each local run. The simplest in-memory pipeline indexed the corpus fastest, with an average indexing time of 17.65 seconds. The Milvus-supported pipelines took longer to index because document chunks and embeddings had to be prepared and stored through the vector-database backend. The reranking and hybrid with reranking pipelines had the longest indexing times in the local results, which reflects the additional setup and component initialisation required by the more complex pipeline configurations.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/fig11_timing_breakdown.png}
    \caption{Average local timing breakdown for indexing, retrieval, and generation across the implemented RAG variants.}
    \label{fig:timing-breakdown}
\end{figure}

Retrieval times were very short for the basic pipeline, the vector-database pipeline, and the tuned vector-database pipeline. The basic pipeline took 0.067 seconds on average, the vector-database pipeline took 0.104 seconds, and the tuned vector-database pipeline took 0.103 seconds. Hybrid retrieval increased average retrieval time to 0.175 seconds. Reranking caused a larger increase because an additional cross-encoder scoring step was applied to the candidate set. The longest retrieval time was produced by the hybrid with reranking pipeline, with an average of 2.988 seconds.

The generation times for each implementation were greater than the retrieval times for each implementation. The vectorDB pipeline had the lowest average generation time with an average of 21.95 seconds and the hybrid with reranking pipeline had the largest average generation time with an average of 35.79 seconds. This indicates that in this local environment, the language-model generation step dominated the per-query response cost. Thus, although retrieval optimization is important, the latency difference was substantially less than that due to generation latency, other than when using a hybrid with reranking pipeline.

\subsection{Local Retrieval and Answer Quality}

Figure~\ref{fig:answer-quality} compares the local answer-quality metrics. The basic pipeline received the highest manual score, with an average of 0.80. The vector-database, tuned vector-database, and hybrid with reranking pipelines each achieved a manual score of 0.60. The reranking pipeline scored 0.50, while the hybrid pipeline had the lowest manual score at 0.40.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/fig09_answer_quality.png}
    \caption{Answer-quality metrics for the local RAG variants, including manual score, ROUGE-L, BERTScore, and semantic similarity.}
    \label{fig:answer-quality}
\end{figure}

The results from using automatic semantic metrics did not always match up perfectly to the manual scores. For example, although the tuned version of the vector-database pipeline got the highest \acs{BERTScore} and semantic-similarity (which both reward how much information overlaps with a given reference-answer) it only received a manual score of 0.60. There are two reasons for this discrepancy. First, semantic similarity only looks at how much information in the generated response overlaps with the information in the reference answer. Second, manual scoring checks whether the answer provides enough operational detail or leaves out required steps. Therefore, an answer can score well semantically while still receiving a lower manual score if it omits required details needed to complete the operation correctly.

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:retrieval-quality}, the vector-database pipeline produced the best results for all retrieval-based metrics. It scored an F1 of 0.451, which represented the highest value for that metric; and it also scored a precision of 0.319, again representing the highest value for that metric. The basic-pipeline scored an F1 of 0.424, and hybrid-reranking scored an F1 of 0.405. All four of those pipelines (basic-pipeline, vector-database pipeline, tuned vector-database pipeline, and hybrid with reranking) scored a perfect 100\% on retrieval-recall. The reranker scored 90\%, and the hybrid-pipeline scored 70\%.

    \caption{Retrieval-quality metrics for the local RAG variants, comparing hit rate, precision, recall, and F1.}

The results from this local test indicate that hybrid pipeline performance did not improve overall search quality. Hybrid search is expected to improve performance as a combination of both lexical and semantic cues can enhance search results. However, neither the Milvus hybrid configuration nor the small number of scenarios used in this study provided a significant improvement over the dense vector-database pipeline.

The hybrid with reranking variant improved retrieval quality in comparison to the hybrid-only variant; however, there was also a large increase in retrieval time.

Manual score-by-scenario in Figure~\ref{fig:scenario-heatmap} shows that scenario 1, \acs{Sev-1} \acs{SLA} Lookup, was the least difficult (average score = 0.92). Scenario 3 and 5 were the most difficult (both averaged 0.42) as they required cross-functional reasoning about onboarding, IT, Logistics, Customer Success, Product and Commercial processes. Low scores suggest that searching for the correct documents is insufficient if the user needs information regarding multiple operationally dependent processes.

\section{HPC Deployment Results}

The \ac{HPC} phase ran the same six \ac{RAG} pipeline architectures (and the five scenario question types) that were used in the local workflow. This section's comparison will be limited to the timing data collected by the two workloads. Because the local and \ac{HPC} workflows are running with differing model-serving backends, and differing backend models for some of them, the comparison will be viewed strictly as a workflow level timing study, and not as a hardware-based benchmark.

For all six pipelines, generation time was significantly better when the \ac{HPC} workflow was deployed. Generation speed-ups compared to the local workflow ranged from 7.390$\times$ for the hybrid pipeline to 10.518$\times$ for the tuned vector-database pipeline. Indexing time was also better for each of the pipelines run; however, the best improvement in indexing speed was seen in the basic pipeline at a 10.164$\times$ speed-up.

Retrieval time did not behave as predictably. The basic, reranking, and hybrid-reranking pipelines performed faster on \ac{HPC} in terms of retrieval time. Conversely, the vector-database, tuned vector-database, and hybrid pipelines performed worse (slower) on \ac{HPC}.

\section{Effectiveness of Retrieval-Level Optimization Techniques}

Local results showed mixed effectiveness for retrieval-level optimization techniques. Dense retrieval using Milvus-backed vectors enhanced local F1 scores compared to the in-memory baseline but did not improve manual answer score. This suggests that storing vectors persistently provides a stronger foundation for retrieving data without always enhancing generative quality.

The tuned vector-database run yielded better \acs{ROUGE-L}, \acs{BERTScore} and semantic similarity than the original vector-database run; however it also decreased local retrieval F1 score. Larger chunks may have given the generator more complete context while also making source-level retrieval less accurate. This supports treating chunk size as an experimental parameter with meaning, rather than just a technical process choice.

Reranking did not enhance local manual score in this particular experiment and caused average retrieval latency to increase from the sub-second range to more than one second on average. The hybrid-reranking pipeline was better than hybrid retrieval alone and equivalent to the manual score of the vector-database variant, but had the longest times for both retrieval and generation. Thus, reranking introduced a clear cost, while its quality benefit was limited for this corpus and scenario set.

The results of the \ac{HPC} timing also make a significant point about the overall (local) level of retrieval performance. The basic pipeline achieved the greatest total speed-up during \ac{HPC} execution; however, this is not to say that the basic pipeline will generate better retrieval quality than the other pipelines; rather, it merely generated the largest benefit in terms of runtime as a result of \ac{HPC} execution. Likewise, both the generation and indexing times were improved by \ac{HPC} on the Milvus-based pipelines, which contributed to their respective improvements in total runtime, whereas the retrieval time in each of the VectorDB, VectorDB-Tuned, and Hybrid pipelines was actually longer than before. In conclusion, these results support the notion that at the retrieval level, optimizations and costs associated with execution are distinct.

\section{Research Questions and Hypotheses}

The results address the research question through isolating the local retrieval/answer-quality findings from the local vs. \ac{HPC} timing findings.

For \textbf{RQ1}, the results show that retrieval techniques have an impact on both retrieval quality and answer quality; however, they are not always positive. The vector-database pipeline produced the highest retrieval F1 value, while the basic pipeline produced the highest manual answer score. Thus, \textbf{H1 is partially supported}: Changing the retrieval pipeline will positively affect both retrieval and answer outcomes; however, hybrid retrieval and reranking did not produce the best overall local results.

As for \textbf{RQ2}, the results confirm a previously established performance trade off. Reranking and hybrid with reranking added to the retrieval latency for each of the basic and vector-database pipelines. Hybrid with reranking outperformed hybrid retrieval as far as retrieval F1 goes, but it also resulted in the longest retrieval time. Thus, \textbf{H2 is supported}: Adding additional retrieval stages increases the retrieval costs. Further, simply increasing the strength of a retrieval component does not automatically lead to faster answers or higher quality scores.

As far as, for \textbf{RQ3} the timing data show that in nearly all of the local pipeline configurations, generation time accounted for most of the overall query time. The retrieval time was a fraction of the generation time for the basic, vector-database, tuned vector-database and hybrid pipelines; however, as reranking was enabled, the retrieval time became more important. Additionally, indexing time differed significantly among pipelines due to more complex configurations requiring further initialization, additional vector storage, etc. Thus, \textbf{H3 is supported}.

Finally, for \textbf{RQ4}, the local versus HPC results indicate that all six pipelines were able to achieve a lower total runtime using \ac{HPC}. As far as total speed-up goes, an average total speed-up of 5.05x can be observed with the largest individual speed-up being associated with the basic pipeline. However, the per-component results demonstrate that \ac{HPC} was not equally beneficial for all of the timing categories. Specifically, for three out of four pipelines (vector-database, tuned vector-database, hybrid pipelines) slower times were noticed for retrieval within HPC. Thus, \textbf{H4 is partially supported}: HPC execution positively impacted total runtime and generation heavy workload; however, it did not positively impact retrieval time for all of the pipeline variations.

\section{Comparison with Literature}

The results confirm literature surveyed in chapter 3; which stated that \ac{RAG} performance is dependent upon both retrieval quality (the accuracy of what has been found) and generation behaviour (how good the generated answers are). On the local results, dense vector retrieval had good results due to the fact that the most likely source of evidence for policy, handbooks, and operation documentation contained semantically related content. But high retrieval scores didn't always correlate with the highest manual answer scores. Therefore, the idea of evaluating \ac{RAG} based on both the quality of the retrieved evidence and the quality of the generated answer has merit.

The reranked results are consistent with the typical tradeoff of increased latency for cross-encoder reranking. Reranking is able to enhance candidate ordering by utilizing a more relevant model once candidates have been selected during the initial retrieval phase. However, reranking introduces another processing phase into the overall pipeline. During this thesis, this additional processing introduced significant increases in the time required to complete the retrieval phase of the pipeline, however, it resulted in limited improvements to manual answer quality when considering a very small corpus size and a very small number of test scenarios.

The results from the hybrid retrieval experiments were somewhat unexpected. Hybrid retrieval is typically presented as a method to provide exact lexical matching combined with semantic retrieval. However, the local results demonstrated that hybrid retrieval will require extensive tuning before it can be reasonably assumed to produce better results than dense retrieval. A variety of factors including query type, sparse vector configurations, document structures and fusion parameterization can all impact the ability of hybrid retrieval to improve results.

The \ac{HPC} timing results support discussions surrounding deployment and model-serving overheads provided in chapter 3. Large generation time improvements demonstrate that the \ac{HPC} serving environment is advantageous for generating heavy workloads. However, slower times for many of the Milvus-based variants demonstrates that \ac{HPC} may not be equally advantageous for all pipeline components. Shorter retrieval operations may result in service interaction times, storage access times or other vector-database overhead times dominating measured times.

\section{Trade-offs and Limitations}

There is one major trade-off involved in this study; namely, a balance between ease of implementation and representation of real-world deployment. The in-memory pipeline (basic) was implemented most quickly and was able to produce the best manual score with the shortest indexing time; however, it is less indicative of a scalable production-based retrieval system. On the other hand, the vector-database pipeline had the longest indexing time, but provided the largest improvement over baseline retrieval (i.e., the strongest retrieval F1) and can serve as an example of a more realistic storage base for larger document collections.

Another important trade-off involves retrieving relevant documents versus increasing the time required for queries. reranking and hybrid with reranking both added steps to evaluate each document's relevance. Although the latter technique improved retrieval quality compared to hybrid retrieval alone, it resulted in the longest retrieval times. Therefore, whether or not to pay this additional cost will depend upon how valuable additional retrieval accuracy will be in a particular application setting.

The results from this thesis have several limitations that restrict their generalizability. First, the corpus used is synthetic and there are only five questions in the question-set used in this study. As such, generalization of these results to all possible scenarios is impossible. Second, manual scoring uses a very simple rubric and therefore likely did not fully account for the nuances of answer quality. Finally, the local results are dependent upon the state of the local runtime environment (e.g., Ollama behaviour, model warm up, background processes, etc.) as well as the Milvus service.

The major limitation of the \ac{HPC} comparison is that it focused solely on runtime. Thus, while it allows for drawing conclusions regarding indexing time, retrieval time, generation time, and total runtime; it does not allow making conclusions regarding answer quality or retrieval quality. Furthermore, the timing summary provided by the local and \ac{HPC} workflows do not separate out queue time, service startup time, data transfer time and detailed hardware utilization. Additionally, the local and \ac{HPC} workflows utilize different serving stacks and some different backend models; therefore the comparisons made here should be considered at a workflow level deployment comparison rather than a pure hardware comparison.

\section{Summary}

This chapter has shown the local and \ac{HPC} results of the six implemented \ac{RAG} pipeline variants. The local analysis revealed there is no single pipeline which achieves the best results at each level (i.e., answer quality, retrieval quality, run-time).

The \ac{HPC} timing comparisons demonstrated that all six pipelines exhibited significantly faster total runtime when executed using \ac{HPC} resources, although the greatest reductions in processing time were observed for the generation time. Conversely, the degree to which each of the pipelines benefited from the use of \ac{HPC} for reducing the amount of time required for retrieving answers varied widely. Moreover, it appears that both the process of accessing stored vectors within databases, as well as the overhead associated with interacting with services can be a significant impediment to rapid retrieval times for relatively short queries.

In summary, the results provide evidence supporting the primary research objective of this thesis that optimizing the performance of retrieval-level \acp{RAG} is contingent upon a variety of trade-offs regarding the quality of answers produced by these systems, the quality of retrievals provided by them, and costs incurred during system execution. Furthermore, the results indicate that executing pipelines via \ac{HPC} methods can potentially reduce overall runtime and may produce greater benefits for those pipelines where generation time dominates overall processing time; however, simply executing pipelines via \ac{HPC} does not guarantee improved performance for each individual component.
